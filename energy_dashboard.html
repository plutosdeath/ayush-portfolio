01 · How we got here
Crisis timeline: February 28 to today
Key events and price reactions
Feb 28
US and Israel launch strikes on Iran. Ayatollah Khamenei killed.
Iran retaliates by blockading the Strait of Hormuz. Iraq declares force majeure. Kuwait cuts output. Tanker traffic through the Strait falls to near zero within 48 hours.
Brent: $65 → $71
Mar 4-7
Gulf producers cut 10 million barrels per day as storage fills.
With nowhere to export, Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait and UAE cut combined output. More than 3 mb/d of regional refining capacity shuts. Iran attacks cargo vessels in the Persian Gulf.
Brent: $84 → $99
Mar 9
Trump hints at peace deal. Retracted Navy tanker escort post moves markets $8.
Brent spikes to $116 intraday on escalation fears, then drops sharply on peace deal signal, then recovers when Energy Secretary's tanker escort post is retracted as inaccurate. Classic sentiment-driven whipsaw.
Intraday: $116 · Settlement: $94
Mar 11
IEA releases 400 million barrels — largest in 52-year history.
32 member nations agree to the unprecedented release. The US commits 172 million barrels from the SPR over 120 days, equivalent to 1.4 mb/d. Markets price the announcement and immediately look past it.
Brent closes above $100 for first time since Aug 2022
Mar 12-17
New supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei vows Hormuz stays closed.
Son of the assassinated Ayatollah, Mojtaba hardens Iran's position. IEA director Birol states the reserve release is a stop-gap. Rystad forecasts Brent at $110 by April if war continues, $135 by June if it extends to four months.
Today: loading...
The 400 million barrel release — the largest in IEA history — covers only 15% of the daily supply gap and takes 120 days to fully deploy. It buys time. It does not solve the crisis. Brent is still $37 above pre-war levels and the risk premium has not cleared.

04 · The structural argument
01 · THE CHOKEPOINT PROBLEM
A 33km strait should not reprice the global economy overnight
The Hormuz closure is not the first time this has happened and it will not be the last. Every year of continued fossil fuel import dependency is another year of exposure to whoever controls the chokepoints. This is not an argument about climate. It is an argument about economic sovereignty.
02 · THE RESERVE PROBLEM
Emergency stockpiles are a stop-gap, not a strategy
The largest reserve release in IEA history covers 15% of the daily gap and takes 120 days to deploy. The US is now burning through reserves built after the 1973 Arab oil embargo. Each crisis draws down the buffer available for the next one. The strategy of holding reserves assumes disruptions are temporary. This one may not be.
03 · THE TRANSITION ACCELERANT
Every supply shock makes the transition case stronger
Oil at $100+ makes European biomethane, wind, solar, and storage cost-competitive almost overnight. Energy security concerns give politicians the mandate for the regulatory frameworks the transition has been waiting for. Crisis historically accelerates structural change in energy. The 1973 embargo created the IEA. This crisis could create the regulatory framework that unlocks European domestic energy at scale.
